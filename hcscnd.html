Title 17 of the United States Code

Chapter 1. Subject Matter and Scope of Copyright

101. Definitions

102. Subject matter of copyright

Copyrights can be granted to original works of authorship fixed in any tangible medium. A tangible medium must be sufficiently permanent or stable to permit it to be perceived, reproduced, or copied.

103. Compilations and derivative works

Compilations and derivative works that meet the standard of 102 are protected by copyright given that they use the preexisting material lawfully. This copyright only protects the added material, not the material on which it was based.

104. National Origin

Unpublished works are subject to full protection of this act regardless of the author’s nationality, published works are protected if published in the US or a treaty party.

104a. Restored Works

Copyrights for works that have been restored will subsist on the date of the restoration and will exist for the remainder of the copyright’s natural term.

105. Government Works

The government cannot hold a copyright for any of its original works, but may hold copyrights transferred to it.

106. Exclusive Rights

A copyright owner has the exclusive rights to copy, distribute, perform and display publicly and/or digitally, and create derivative works of the copyrighted work. 106a. Rights of Attribution and Integrity The exclusive rights holder of a visual work has the right to claim authorship or prevent the use of their name on a work they did not create. Authors may also prevent the use of their name in the event of a mutilation of their work, and in some cases prevent any intentional mutilation.

107. Fair Use

Violation of the rights in section 106 for the purpose of criticism, comment, news, academia, research, parody, or use of a nominal portion of the work(de minimis taking) is does not infringe the copyright. Fair use is intentionally broad and is often decided on a case-by-case basis.

108. Reproduction by libraries and archives

For the purposes of preservation, libraries and archives may make a single copy of a copyrighted work without infringing the copyright, so long as this is done without a purpose of commercial gain and the library or archive are open to the public, specifically researchers.

109. Transferring of copies

The owner of a copy of a work may sell or otherwise dispose of that copy without permission from the copyright owner. However, an individual sound recording or computer program may not rent, lease, or lend that copy for commercial gain of any form.

110. Exemption of certain performances and displays

Performance or display of a copyrighted work for education, private screening, government screening, public display intended for promotion, screening for the sake of the disabled or handicapped, and social functions for veterans are not copyright infringements, so long as the event does not seek commercial gain and uses a legal copy of the work.

111. Secondary transmissions of broadcast programming by cable

Secondary transmission of a performance or display of a work embodied in a primary transmission is not an infringement of copyright if the transmission is not directly controlled by the cable system, exempted by section 110, or covered by a statutory license. Does not apply if the primary transmission is exempted by section 110 in a way that the secondary transmission is not.

112. Ephemeral Recordings

Organizations may transmit and make a single copy of a performance or display of work under a license, except in the case of motion picture or other audiovisual works. Transmission groups also may be given statutory licenses for their recordings under this section, provided it is used only for transmission. Nonprofits and the government are given further leniency in certain cases.

113. Scope of exclusive rights in pictorial, graphic, and sculptural works

114. Scope of exclusive rights in sound recordings

The owner of a sound recording copyright is not entitled to publicly perform that work, but may play the recording publicly. The owner is entitled to duplicate, remix, or rearrange the sound recording, but may not make a duplicate of independent sounds in order to simulate the original. Owners may independently negotiate statutory licenses for their sound recordings.

115. Scope of exclusive rights in nondramatic musical works

Exclusive licenses to copy and distribute nondramatic musical works are subject to compulsory licensing by individuals who have lawfully obtained a copy of the work. Licensed works may be changed to fit a new format but cannot change the underlying character of the work, and the attempt to obtain a compulsory license must occur at least thirty days after the making of copies and before any distribution of the work.

116. Licenses for coin-operated record players

Owners of copyrights and record players may negotiate the terms and royalty rates for use of copyrighted works in coin-operated record players in lieu of any determination otherwise by the Copyright Royalty Judges. Others will have rates and terms set according to Chapter 8.

117. Computer Programs

118. Use of certain works in connection with noncommercial broadcasting

Copyright owners and public broadcasting entities may negotiate and agree upon terms and royalty rates in lieu of any determination by the Copyright Royalty Judges if filed within 30 days of execution, and may designate common agents to negotiate, agree to, pay, or receive payments.

119. Secondary transmission of distant television programming by satellite

Secondary transmissions of a performance or display of a work embodied in a primary transmissions by both network and non-network station shall be subject to statutory licensing if they are made to the public for private viewing or for viewing in a commercial establishment, compliant with the related FCC rules, and does so for direct or indirect commercial gain.
